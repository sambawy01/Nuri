Classmates ask questions and offer feedback.\n- Mr. Helmy meets individually with each student to help narrow to one topic.\n- Examples of strong capstone topics:\n  - \"Is the seagrass bed near our school shrinking? A two-season comparison.\"\n  - \"How much plastic enters the water from the beach in front of [hotel name] during peak tourist season vs. off-season?\"\n  - \"Do fish communities differ between the reef section with mooring buoys and the section where boats still anchor?\"\n  - \"Can a simple educational sign at the beach entry reduce tourist touching of coral? A before-and-after study.\"\n  - \"What is the species diversity of the Hamata mangrove creek compared to the open reef nearby?\"\n  - \"How do local fishermen's catch records compare to five years ago? An oral history project.\"\n\n**Weeks 6-8: Background Research**\n- Students read simplified versions of relevant scientific papers (provided by Mr. Helmy).\n- They research their topic using approved websites, park management documents, and interviews with knowledgeable adults.\n- They write a \"Background\" section: What is already known about this topic?\n\n**Weeks 9-10: Hypothesis and Method Design**\n- Each student formulates a clear, testable hypothesis (or, for observational studies, a clear research question).\n- They design their data collection method with Mr. Helmy's guidance, including: what to measure, where, when, how often, and what equipment they need.\n- Mr. Helmy reviews each method for safety, feasibility, and scientific validity.\n\n**Weeks 11-12: Method Practice**\n- Students conduct a \"practice run\" of their data collection to identify problems.\n- They refine their method based on what they learned.\n- They write a \"Methods\" section.\n\n**Deliverable at End of Term 1:** Project Proposal — a written document containing title, background, hypothesis/question, methods, timeline, and materials needed. Presented to the class for peer review.\n\n---\n\n#### Term 2: Data Collection and Analysis (Weeks 13-24)\n\n**Weeks 13-20: Data Collection**\n- Students collect data according to their plan. Most projects require multiple collection sessions.\n- Mr. Helmy checks in weekly: Is the method working? Is data being recorded properly? Any problems?\n- Students maintain a field journal documenting every data collection session — what they did, conditions, observations, any deviations from the plan.\n\n**Weeks 21-22: Data Organization**\n- Students compile all data into organized tables.\n- They create at least two types of graphs appropriate for their data.\n- Mr. Helmy teaches them to calculate basic statistics relevant to their project.\n\n**Weeks 23-24: Analysis and Interpretation**\n- Students analyze their data: Do the results support or refute their hypothesis?\n- They identify patterns, trends, surprises, and limitations.\n- They write \"Results\" and \"Discussion\" sections.\n\n**Deliverable at End of Term 2:** Mid-Project Report — complete with data tables, graphs, and preliminary analysis. Presented to the class for feedback.\n\n---\n\n#### Term 3: Solutions, Action, and Presentation (Weeks 25-36)\n\n**Weeks 25-27: Solution Design**\n- Based on their findings, students propose a realistic, implementable solution or recommendation.\n- Solutions must be specific, justified by evidence, and appropriate to the local context.\n- Example: \"Based on my data showing that the beach in front of [hotel] accumulates X kg of plastic per week during peak season, I recommend the hotel install three additional waste bins at these specific locations, add signage in Arabic and English, and conduct a weekly staff cleanup.\"\n\n**Weeks 28-30: Implementation**\n- Students implement what they can. Not every project will result in a physical change — some will produce reports, educational materials, or recommendations.\n- Examples of implementation:\n  - A student whose project was about tourist behavior creates and installs (with permission) an educational sign.\n  - A student whose project was about seagrass decline shares their data with the park management office.\n  - A student whose project was about fish diversity creates a species guide for a local dive center.\n  - A student whose project was about plastic pollution organizes a targeted cleanup and presents data to the hotel manager.\n\n**Weeks 31-33: Writing and Preparation**\n- Students write the final sections: \"Conclusions\" and \"Recommendations.\"\n- They compile the full report in scientific format: Title, Abstract, Introduction, Background, Methods, Results, Discussion, Conclusions, Recommendations, References.\n- They prepare a presentation with visual aids (poster, slides on tablet, or physical display).\n\n**Weeks 34-35: Practice Presentations**\n- Students present to classmates and receive structured feedback.\n- They revise and polish.\n\n**Week 36: Capstone Exhibition**\n- Public presentation event. Audience includes: other students, parents, teachers, and invited community members (hotel managers, dive instructors, park rangers, local officials).\n- Each student presents for 10-15 minutes, followed by 5 minutes of questions.\n- Mr. Helmy introduces each student, highlighting their growth over the year.\n\n**Deliverable at End of Term 3:** Final Report, Presentation, and Reflection Essay.\n\n---\n\n### Assessment Rubric for Capstone Project\n\n| Criterion | Excellent (4) | Proficient (3) | Developing (2) | Beginning (1) |\n|---|---|---|---|---|\n| **Scientific Method** | All steps clearly applied;
